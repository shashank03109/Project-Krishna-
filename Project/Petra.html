PETRA

Petra, ancient city, center of an Arab kingdom in Hellenistic and Roman times, the ruins of which are in southwest Jordan. The city was built on a terrace, pierced from east to west by the Wadi Mūsā (the Valley of Moses)—one of the places where, according to tradition, the Israelite leader Moses struck a rock and water gushed forth. The valley is enclosed by sandstone cliffs veined with shades of red and purple varying to pale yellow, and for this reason Petra was called by the 19th-century English biblical scholar John William Burgon a “rose-red city half as old as Time.” The modern town of Wadi Mūsā, situated adjacent to the ancient city, chiefly serves the steady stream of tourists who continue to visit the site.

1. More About It

The Greek name Petra (“Rock”) probably replaced the biblical name Sela. Remains from the Paleolithic and Neolithic periods have been discovered at Petra, and Edomites are known to have occupied the area about 1200 bce. Centuries later the Nabataean, an Arab tribe, occupied it and made it the capital of their kingdom. In 312 bce the region was attacked by Seleucid forces, who failed to seize the city. Under Nabataean rule, Petra prospered as a centre of the spice trade that involved such disparate realms as China, Egypt, Greece, and India, and the city’s population swelled to between 10,000 and 30,000.

When the Nabataeans were defeated by the Romans in 106 ce, Petra became part of the Roman province of Arabia but continued to flourish until changing trade routes caused its gradual commercial decline. After an earthquake (not the first) damaged the city in 551, significant habitation seems to have ceased. The Islamic invasion occurred in the 7th century, and a Crusader outpost is evidence of activity there in the 12th century. After the Crusades, the city was unknown to the Western world until it was rediscovered by the Swiss traveler Johann Ludwig Burckhardt in 1812.

2.How It Was Constructed ?

Petra was a city built into the natural landscape of southwest Jordan by the Nabataeans, an Arab tribe known for their skill in architecture and engineering. The city’s most impressive feature is its rock-cut architecture, with buildings, tombs, and monuments carved directly into the cliffs of red sandstone. The Nabataeans designed an advanced water management system, including dams, cisterns, and channels, to capture and store water, making the city sustainable in a desert environment. The city was divided into several districts, with public spaces, temples, and residential areas connected by a network of winding paths.

One of Petra’s most iconic elements is the Siq, a narrow gorge that serves as the main entrance. Buildings like the Treasury (Al-Khazneh) and the Monastery (Ad Deir) are carved into the cliffs and feature a blend of Hellenistic and local architectural styles. Petra thrived as a center of trade, connecting important routes between the Arabian Peninsula, Egypt, and the Mediterranean.

Petra’s construction also involved building road networks and aqueducts that connected distant regions and provided water throughout the city. These innovations enabled the thriving urban center to flourish in the desert, supporting trade, agriculture, and cultural exchange between various civilizations, contributing to economic prosperity and socio-political stability..

3. What Does This Statue Symbolise ?

Petra symbolizes the ingenuity of the Nabataeans, blending architecture with nature. It represents cultural prosperity, ancient trade routes, and the rise and fall of civilizations, standing as a historical testament.
