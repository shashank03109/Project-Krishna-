THE COLOSSEUM

Colosseum, giant amphitheater built in Rome under the Flavian emperors. Construction of the Colosseum was begun sometime between 70 and 72 CE during the reign of Vespasian. It is located just east of the Palatine Hill, on the grounds of what was Nero’s Golden House. The artificial lake that was the centerpiece of that palace complex was drained, and the Colosseum was sited there, a decision that was as much symbolic as it was practical. Vespasian, whose path to the throne had relatively humble beginnings, chose to replace the tyrannical emperor’s private lake with a public amphitheater that could host tens of thousands of Romans.

The structure was officially dedicated in 80 ce by Titus in a ceremony that included 100 days of games. Later, in 82 ce, Domitian completed the work by adding the uppermost story.

1. More About It

The Colosseum is a freestanding structure of stone and concrete, using a complex system of barrel vaults and groin vaults and measuring 620 by 513 feet (189 by 156 meters) overall. Three of the arena’s stories are encircled by arcades framed on the exterior by engaged columns in the Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian orders; the structure’s rising arrangement of columns became the basis of the Renaissance codification known as the assemblage of orders. The main structural framework and facade are travertine, the secondary walls are volcanic tufa, and the inner bowl and the arcade vaults are concrete.

The amphitheater seated some 50,000 spectators, who were shielded from the sun by a massive retractable velarium (awning). Supporting masts extended from corbels built into the Colosseum’s top, or attic, story, and hundreds of Roman sailors were required to manipulate the rigging that extended and retracted the velarium. The Colosseum was the scene of thousands of hand-to-hand combats between gladiators, of contests between men and animals, and of many larger combats, including mock naval engagements. However, it is uncertain whether the arena was the site of the martyrdom of early Christians.

2.How It Was Constructed ?

The construction of the Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheater, began in 70 CE under Emperor Vespasian and was completed in 80 CE by his son Titus. The structure was built on the site of Nero’s Golden House and the artificial lake that was its centerpiece. The decision to construct the Colosseum on this land was both practical and symbolic: it transformed a private imperial space into a public arena, emphasizing the return of power to the people after Nero’s rule.

The Colosseum’s foundation was one of the first major engineering feats, involving a large, deep structure that supported the immense weight of the amphitheater. The foundation was made from stone and concrete, with a core of volcanic tufa and travertine, which was quarried nearby. The material choices allowed for durability and flexibility, crucial for such a large-scale building.

The building used an advanced system of barrel vaults and groin vaults to distribute weight across the structure. These architectural elements allowed the Colosseum to be freestanding without the need for external support, unlike many other Roman buildings. The use of concrete in the inner structure and the vaulting system was innovative, giving the Colosseum its distinctive shape and making it more resistant to earthquakes.

The Colosseum’s façade featured three stories of arcades, each framed by columns in different architectural styles: Doric on the first level, Ionic on the second, and Corinthian on the third.

The entire structure was completed with a final layer of decorative elements, including statues and sculptures that adorned the outer walls and interior. The Colosseum not only served as a place of entertainment but also symbolized the power and might of the Roman Empire, reflecting the grandeur and strength of the empire itself.

3. What Does This Statue Symbolise ?

The Colosseum in Rome, Italy symbolizes the power, wealth, and brutality of the Roman Empire. It also represents the Flavian dynasty that built it, and the social cohesion that the Colosseum brought to the Roman people.
